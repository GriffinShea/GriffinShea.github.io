The objectie of our project was to do the same thing only that rather than programming the ants’ behaviour manually, we would use neural networks and an evolutionary algorithm and see whether the ants would learn these behaviours on their own. The final result of our work is this:


The Simulation:

The first thing to do was setup our simulation. The world our ants inhabit is defined by the World class and looks like this:

At the beginning of the simulation, the ants are the little black pixels hanging around the edge of the hive, which is the pink square section at the center of the grid. Piles of food are dropped randomly around the edge of world (shown in green). Each step in the simulation, the ants gather information from their surroundings, think, and take actions. When an ant wanders over a food pixel, it picks up the food and turns white to show it is carrying food. When an ant carrying food walks onto the hive, it turns black again, to show it has delivered the food. They also have a capability to lay down two types of signals – A and B – for their friends to smell. One space in the grid can hold both an A and a B signal. Each signal carries a value from -1 to 1. In the image, the A signal determines the redness of the pixel and the B signal determines the blueness of the pixel.

The Ants Sense, Think, and Act:

A neural network is a computational model inspired by nature. They are a simple yet powerful tool in artificial intelligence. We use them to control intelligent agents, such as our ants who sense, think, and act each step of the simulation. The ants’ neural networks are defined by the following diagram:

Sensing:

On the left are the sensory, or input, neurons, which define what an ant senses about its environment:

  • checkSignalA/checkSignalB --> tells the ant whether or not it is standing on a signal (-1 or 1)
  • readSignalA/readSignalB --> tells the ant the value of the signals it is standing on (-1 to 1)
  • checkWall --> tells the ant if the space in front of it is a wall (-1 or 1)
  • hasFood --> tells the ant whether it is carrying food (-1 or 1)
  • direction --> tells the ant what direction it is facing (8 directions normalized to -1 to 1)
  • RNG --> a random number from -1 to 1 (simulating random stimuli)
  • STP --> how far along the simulation is, normalized from -1 at the start of the simulation to 1 at the end of the simulation (kind of like the age of the ant)
  • OSC --> a value oscillating from -1 to 1 every 20 steps (simulating the ant's internal clock)

Thinking:

Thinking for our ants means computing the neural network. This involves feeding the values from input through the hidden layer and finally to the actuator, or output layer. The values of the input neurons are set during the sensing step. Then, the hidden neuron values are calculated. Our neural network is fully connected, meaning each node in one layer connects to each node in the next layer. Each node connection has an associated weight with a value determined by the ant’s genome, which will be explained more later. It looks like this:

Acting:

Once the actuator nodes have been calculated, we simply read these values to determine the ant’s actions for that step of the simulation. There are only seven things they can do, as we consider picking up and delivering food to the hive as automatic.

  • dropSignalA/dropSignalB --> if value>0, drop A/B signal
  • signalValueA/signalValueB --> determines the value of a dropped A/B signal
  • turnCW: if value>0 --> rotate the ant’s direction clockwise
  • turnCCW: if value>0 --> rotates the ant’s direction counterclockwise
  • moveForward: if value>0 --> move the ant forward one space in it’s current direction

Training: The Genome, The Evolutionary Algorithm - Natural Selection

What remains to be explained is how we set the weights in the genome in order for the ants to behave properly and accomplish their objective of collecting food and bringing it to the hive. We could set them manually to achieve the desired effect, but for a complicated neural network, that method is not typically feasible. Plus, if we were to change the ants’ sensors, actuators, objective, or environment, by, for example, building walls in the world to block their path or adding predators, we may have to redesign the entire set of weights. We want them to be adaptable. Besides, it’s more fun to watch them learn. Therefore, we will use a method of machine learning to train the ants called an evolutionary algorithm.

For the purpose of this evolutionary algorithm, we call the set of weights a genome. In this project, the genome is saved as a 136-character string, each character representing a weight ranging from -4 to 4 in increments of 0.5. It must be translated like so:

Genetic training will involve placing the ants into the simulation, each with its own random genome or a genome of all 0s. Then, we run the simulation and award points to the ants that achieve the desired behaviours. This becomes their fitness score. An ant gets 1 point for picking up a piece of food and 2 points for delivering it to the hive. The evolutionary algorithm is in main.cpp:

At the end of the simulation, we kill off the ants that performed the worst, saving only the genomes of the best performing ants which are used to create a next generation. Think natural selection.

In order to prevent the gene pool from getting stale, we also add a couple of random mutations. It should be obvious that the ants would never get any better at accomplishing their task if we simply saved the best genome. That's why we need to mutate them somehow in order for them to get better. Another method we could use is mixing genes together, called crossover, which mimics sexual reproduction, but mutation is essential for the ants to develop new behaviours. Running The Simulation:

Watching the video, you will see the ants training. Every 10 generations, we get to see what they’re up to. Quickly they develop wandering behaviours and start putting down signals. It’s quite amusing to watch them paint over the frame. Eventually, after 80 generations they begin to lock into a strategy which involves running in straight lines to the edge of the world and then following the perimeter, where they are sure to find some food. It looks like some of them learn to turn when they run over a trail left by another ant and this allows them to cover most of the world like a canvas, avoiding areas that have already been searched. When bringing food back to the hive, rather than following signals, it looks like their best strategy is to reach one of the corners and dart across the diagonal of the world like a highway, because the hive is always in the center. However, they rarely ever reach a cumulative fitness score above 30.

Moving Forward - Room for Improvement:

There are several ways in which we can make improvements upon the initial iteration of this project. An obvious flaw is that the food is always at the edge of the world, which encourages the running around the edge behaviour. The ants also never learn to plant signals the way we intended and there is a good reason why – it’s not directly incentivised by the fitness score. Let’s imagine an ant was able to follow another ant’s trail to a food source. The ant placing the signals does not get rewarded for that behaviour, even if the second ant will eventually reach the food. We may need to implement some sort of altruistic reward for placing signals. They will also all need to find some sort of agreement on the meaning for signals. Whether that can be trained via the evolutionary algorithm as it is currently setup is hard to say.

Another flaw is the encoding for the ant’s current direction as a sensory node. The direction of the ant is a value from 0 to 7 (representing 8 cardinal directions), but it is translated into a range between -1 and 1, meaning that at some point in turning the value for that input node jumps from -1 to 1 (or vice versa). A better method would be to use two input nodes, one representing the North-South direction of the ant, and the other representing the East-West direction of the ant. It should also be noted that the actuator nodes tell the ant whether it should turn clockwise or counterclockwise, which doesn’t really map to cardinal directions.

There are several other ways that improvements could be made. The ants can only smell the pheromone on their space, but in future we could allow them to smell the spaces adjacent to them as well. We could also make a number of modifications to the neural network itself. Adding more layers of hidden nodes could allow for more complicated thinking and behaviours. We could also add memory by giving the hidden nodes a weighted connection to themselves, carrying over the value from the previous simulation step. We can also swap out the activation function and see if it makes a difference.
